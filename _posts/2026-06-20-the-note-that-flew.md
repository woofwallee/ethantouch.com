title: "The Note That Flew"
date: 2026-06-20 09:00:00 -0500
description: "On overheard moments at a school appreciation event, the note that blew away before it reached anybody, and the people we owe gratitude to but never tell."
---

Kids get little slips at school appreciation events. They say to hand them to the teacher who makes you feel seen. Each kid takes a stack and walks around campus to decide. You can almost watch them weighing the choice.

Who actually does that for me? Who do I owe this to? You hear small conversations if you stand close enough. A kid leans in and thanks a teacher for always believing in them.

Then a gust of wind grabs one of the notes. It catches on a chain-link fence and just hangs there. Nobody sees where it was heading, and nobody comes back for it. The kid who wrote it doesn't know it missed the mark.

I keep thinking about that lost note. People usually take a wholesome lesson away from these events. They say kids are good and we should tell people we love them. That isn't the lesson I'm taking home.

Most of our gratitude actually looks just like that fence. We all have a specific person in our head. Maybe it's the friend who showed up the day everything fell apart. Maybe it's the boss who quietly said you're better than you thought.

We owe these people something real, but we never tell them. It isn't because we don't mean it. We just don't make the trip. We tell ourselves they probably already know.

We worry it'd be weird to bring it up years later. We assume they don't need a message about something from a decade ago. But most of them don't know. They wouldn't find it weird at all.

A kid hands a teacher a slip and it changes their whole week. Years later, the teacher still keeps that slip on a desk. The kid had no idea and probably moved on by Friday. That's how the math works.

An action that feels small to you can be enormous for them. That lost note is what most of our adult lives look like. It holds words we meant for people we owed something to. Then life happens, and the note ends up nowhere.

You don't have to wait for someone to hand you a prompt. You can just find the person. Write the note and actually hand it over.
