About

Ethan Touch

Most teams know the frustration: broken workflows, platforms that don't talk to each other, processes that somehow got more complicated every time someone tried to fix them. I've spent over seven years in marketing and marketing technology, and the through line has always been the same. I like figuring things out. Give me a tangled system and I'll find the clean path through it.

HubSpot is my platform of choice. I know it well enough to build complex workflows, train teams from scratch, and push it further than most orgs realize it can go. But my background isn't limited to one tool. I've worked across stacks, inherited legacy platforms, and spent real time in the weeds learning how different systems behave when they're forced to coexist.

What I'm good at is reducing friction. If a process takes ten steps, I find a way to do it in three. If an organization is running on outdated tools that don't integrate with anything modern, I'm not looking to pile more software on top. I'm looking for the shortest, cleanest path from A to B. That usually means researching what already exists, finding the right off-the-shelf solution, and making it fit. Not every problem needs a custom build. Most just need someone willing to dig in and find what works.

I stay sharp the same way outside of work: studying systems, staying curious, and paying attention to where technology is headed. AI, automation, whatever's moving fast. I like understanding it before I need to.

Originally from the South, now holding it down in the Midwest with two dogs, good food, and the occasional chaotic karaoke night.

Challenge

Upstream USA works with hospitals and clinics on contraceptive care. Leads came in from healthcare conferences, webinars, paid campaigns, forms, and content downloads. High volume, multiple sources, all year.

There was no reliable system to sort any of it. Someone had to review spreadsheets manually and figure out whether each contact was a new lead, an existing partner, or not qualified. That took hours, sometimes days. By the time follow-up happened, the lead was often cold.

HubSpot was live, but it was not connected in a useful way to Salesforce, which the recruitment team relied on every day.

Approach

I built five routing workflows, one for each lead source: conferences, webinars, paid media, forms, and content downloads. The logic stayed consistent, but each workflow had its own entry point and source rules. Every lead was tagged, sorted, and routed into the right drip campaign. Custom properties mapped cleanly to Salesforce so both systems stayed aligned.

Each contact was classified automatically at entry. MQL or SQL, lifecycle stage, source, and lead score were all assigned without manual review. I also captured first-touch context so the team knew what brought the contact in before anyone reached out.

I built in duplicate handling too. If a lead came from an organization Upstream already worked with, the workflow caught it, updated the record, and labeled it correctly. That prevented overlapping outreach and messy contact histories.

MQLs and SQLs synced into Salesforce in real time. SQLs entered drip campaigns with Calendly links so scheduling could happen immediately. Bookings triggered notifications and were logged to the contact record. Before that, follow-up was mostly forwarded emails and crossed fingers.

I also built and deployed email templates through HubSpot, managed targeting and suppression logic, handled A/B testing, and set up reporting. Each department got a campaign structure it could actually use, plus a trained owner and a clear path for updates.

Lead imports got cleaned up too. I defined data requirements up front, created a shared intake process, and made the handoff from collection to formatting to import much more reliable.

Because HubSpot's reporting tier was limited, I built a custom tracking model against historical performance. It became the reporting system the team used for meetings and year-end review.

Results
Nearly all manual sorting removed, from hours or days down to near instant
Same-day lead response where follow-up used to lag by days
Dozens of workflows built across routing, drip campaigns, data hygiene, and operational support
Clear pipeline visibility across MQL and SQL stages
Calendly-based scheduling replaced manual email forwarding
Stronger engagement through timely, source-specific messaging
Cleaner segmentation through automated tagging by source, lifecycle stage, and company
Better alignment across marketing, recruitment, and operations

Challenge

The program supported educators through well-being and professional development services. Membership data lived across disconnected tools with no central database. Every new enrollment meant manual tracking, manual group assignment, manual onboarding, and too much bouncing between spreadsheets and platforms.

Nothing was connected. Signup, scheduling, payments, and communication all required handoffs and workarounds. The team wanted to grow, but the system underneath them could not support it.

Approach

I started with a full stack audit: staff interviews, workflow mapping, and a review of every platform in use. HubSpot was the best foundation for the job because it could centralize member data and run automation across the organization.

I connected nine platforms into one working system: Squarespace, Hivebrite, Circles, Zoom, Google Sheets, Gmail, Shopify, ReCharge, and SurveyMonkey. Some pieces used native integrations, others ran through Zapier, but the point was the same: one connected operating model instead of nine isolated tools.

I migrated member data out of legacy systems and spreadsheets into HubSpot, then rebuilt the contact structure with custom properties that fit how the program actually worked.

I also led the move to Shopify with ReCharge for subscription-based memberships. That system synced back into HubSpot so membership status, activity, and communication could stay connected.

From there, I built three core workflows: a registration and welcome series for new members, automated meeting reminders with personalized time slots and group details, and lifecycle communication that carried members through the full program.

I designed the email system around templates, automation, and clear branding so the team could stop sending everything by hand.

Once the system was live, I trained the Millennium Forum team, created documentation, and made sure they could run day-to-day operations on their own. I stayed available for support, but the system was built for independence.

HubSpot's reporting tier still had limits, so I built a custom spreadsheet model for open rates, engagement, bounces, readability, and list performance, all benchmarked historically. That became the team's main reporting tool for internal reviews and annual planning.

Nine platforms unified with HubSpot as the center of the system

Challenge

HubSpot was connected to Salesforce, but it was not being used like a real marketing platform. Salesforce handled recruitment and donor work. HubSpot was mostly functioning as a loose email tool without solid segmentation, repeatable campaign structure, or dependable reporting.

The team was managing recurring newsletters, webinar invites, announcements, and outreach across a large database. Too much of the work was manual. Lists were rebuilt from scratch, assets were recreated, and fields had to be double-checked before each send. Reporting was limited to whatever the subscription tier happened to show.

Approach

I built custom HTML and CSS newsletter templates tailored to each audience while keeping the structure and branding consistent underneath. That gave the team speed without sacrificing quality. Instead of rebuilding emails every month, they worked from a stable system.

I standardized the campaign process across targeting, suppression, testing, QA, and post-send review. Recurring sends and one-off sends followed the same logic. The process stopped living in one person's head.

I built data hygiene workflows in HubSpot to clean records, standardize fields, and make audience logic more reliable. I also reorganized property structure and rebuilt segmentation rules so the lists could be trusted.

Since HubSpot's native reporting was not deep enough, I created a custom performance model covering open rates, click rates, engagement trends, bounces, unsubscribes, and historical comparisons. Reporting prep went from a manual slog to a much faster, cleaner process, and the team had a better way to review performance over time.

I also documented the operation in detail. More than 50 SOPs covered campaign workflows, template usage, segmentation rules, QA standards, and reporting expectations. The team ended up with an actual operating model, not just a collection of workarounds.

Email Performance Analysis

A year of email data across three audiences, and nobody could say what was actually working. I pulled the numbers, benchmarked them against industry averages, and built a dashboard that made the priorities obvious.

Challenge

The organization was emailing three distinct audiences: SQLs, customers, and MQLs. Each group behaved differently, but nobody had looked at them side by side or compared them against outside benchmarks. Most decisions were still being made on instinct.

Open rates were rising, but read rates were falling. Glance rates were up across every segment. People were opening emails without actually reading them. Without clear analysis, the team kept leaning on longer email copy that readers were not finishing.

They also had no real frame of reference. A number might look fine in isolation, but was it actually competitive? Was the click rate strong, average, or weak for a nonprofit of that size? There was no clear answer.

Approach

I pulled a full year of performance data across all three segments, including open rates, click rates, click-to-open rates, bounce rates, unsubscribe rates, read rates, and glance rates. Every metric was broken out by segment and compared year over year.

Then I benchmarked those numbers against national nonprofit averages from Neon One, MailerLite, M+R Benchmarks, and Nonprofit Tech for Good. Each metric was evaluated as above, at, or below benchmark so the team could see not just the number, but what the number meant.

I built an interactive dashboard in Chart.js with segment filters, live-updating KPI cards, and benchmark bands layered onto the charts. It was a single HTML file, lightweight and easy to use.

From there, I turned the analysis into six prioritized actions. The highest-priority fixes were cleaning the customer list, shortening email copy, and improving subject line performance for the weakest segment. The next layer was CTA optimization, send cadence review, and studying the content patterns behind the strongest engagement.

Results
Full benchmark scorecard across five core metrics for all three segments
Interactive dashboard with segment filtering, year-over-year comparison, and benchmark overlays
Clear reading-behavior shift identified, with read rates down and glance rates up across all segments
Top segment CTOR landed at 1.5x benchmark, validating content strategy for that audience
Six prioritized actions tied directly to benchmark gaps
List hygiene issue flagged before it could do more damage to deliverability

Challenge

Twenty-eight pages of legal analysis: protective order outcomes, judge-by-judge variation, dismissal patterns, petitioner support, methodology, and policy recommendations. Dense, important, and completely undesigned. It needed to hold up under scrutiny and still be something a normal person could actually sit down and read.

The writing was already done. Nobody needed me to touch the analysis. What they needed was someone to take all of that content and make it look like the serious report it actually was.

That meant charts, section breaks, callout boxes, photography, and a layout system that could carry heavy content without burying the reader in it.

Approach

I designed the full report from scratch. Not a template drop. Every page was built around the content it was carrying, with a consistent system tying the whole thing together.

That system included a cover, section headers, recurring page layouts, chart pages, callout boxes, photography placement, and recommendation pages. Each section followed the same design logic so the report felt like one piece, not 28 separate pages.

The heaviest sections, methodology, findings, discussion, recommendations, got the most attention. I broke long blocks into scannable layouts, pulled key data into charts and callouts, and made sure the most important patterns were visible without having to dig for them.

Once it was built, I prepped it for publishing and distribution. The final version was ready to post, share, and hand to stakeholders without any additional work.

Nonprofit Leadership Alliance

Challenge

The page had a solid foundation. It explained the program, captured leads, and gave students a clear next step. But it was leaving conversions on the table.

Tracking was incomplete. One email capture path had a broken integration. The sign-up flow had an extra confirmation step adding unnecessary friction. On mobile, the layout broke in places and copy got hard to read. The form had fields that made the data messy on the back end.

The page didn't need a redesign. It needed someone to go through it carefully, find the gaps, and tell the team exactly what to fix.

Approach

I audited the page across five areas: tracking, lead capture, form experience, messaging, and mobile performance.

I started with tracking. The conversion setup needed simplification, one sign-up path was missing a conversion signal entirely, and an integration issue was breaking email capture. Those were the highest-priority fixes.

Then I looked at the sign-up flow. An extra confirmation step was adding friction. The form had fields that needed to be simplified or standardized so the data coming in was cleaner and the experience was faster to complete.

On mobile, the page had real problems. Spacing was off, readability dropped, and parts of the layout broke. Desktop was fine. Mobile was not.

I also went through the messaging. Some of the copy needed tightening, a few elements were adding clutter, and the value of the program needed to land earlier in the page.

Last recommendation: audience-specific landing page variants. Instead of sending all traffic to one generic page, build versions that match the visitor. Same core offer, more relevant framing.

Results
Delivered a full landing page audit for Nonprofit Leadership Alliance
Audited a live campaign page supporting Alpha Phi Omega outreach
Identified tracking and lead capture issues that could affect conversion measurement
Flagged sign-up friction that could reduce form completion or list growth
Surfaced mobile usability issues affecting readability, spacing, and layout
Recommended form updates to improve usability and data consistency
Strengthened message clarity by reducing clutter and sharpening the offer
Added a performance layer to the audit, including desktop and mobile page speed review
Created a practical optimization roadmap the team could use without starting over
