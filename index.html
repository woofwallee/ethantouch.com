Lead Response Time
Challenge

Upstream USA works with hospitals and clinics on contraceptive care. They pulled leads from 5 to 10 healthcare conferences a year, sometimes 1,000+ contacts per event. Add webinars, paid campaigns, form fills, white paper downloads. Thousands of leads annually, coming in from everywhere.

No system to sort any of it. Someone sat with a spreadsheet and manually cross-referenced every contact: new lead, existing partner, or disqualified. That took hours. Sometimes days. By the time anyone followed up, the lead had gone cold.

HubSpot was live but not connected to Salesforce, which the 10+ person recruitment team used every day.

Approach

Five routing workflows. One per channel: conferences, webinars, paid media, forms, content downloads. Same logic, different entry points. Each one tagged, sorted, and routed leads into channel-specific drip campaigns. 60+ custom properties mapped to Salesforce kept data clean across both platforms.

Every contact got classified automatically. MQL or SQL. Lifecycle stage assigned. Source tagged. Lead scored. Custom properties captured the first interaction, so when the team reached out, they already had context.

Duplicate detection built in. If a lead came from a company Upstream already worked with, the workflow caught it. Record updated. Tagged as existing. No overlapping messages. No contradictions.

MQLs and SQLs synced to Salesforce in real time. SQLs entered drip campaigns, 3 to 6 emails, with Calendly for scheduling. Bookings triggered notifications and logged to the contact record. The old way was forwarding emails and hoping someone followed up.

Email templates designed in Beefree. Deployed through HubSpot. Subject lines, targeting, suppression, A/B testing, post-send reporting. Each department's drip campaign built and handed off to a trained owner. Clear path back for updates.

Lead list imports got a process. Data requirements defined upfront. Shared inbox for visibility. Clear handoff from collection to formatting to import. No more guessing who had the list.

HubSpot's reporting tier wasn't deep enough. So I built a custom tracking model: open rates, engagement, bounces, readability, unsubscribes, all benchmarked against historical data. It became the team's go-to for meetings and year-end reviews. The CEO read the annual report.

Results
99% reduction in manual lead vetting, from hours/days to near-instant sorting
24-hour lead response where there used to be significant delays
50+ workflows built across lead routing, drip campaigns, data hygiene, and operations
Full pipeline visibility across MQL/SQL stages for all teams
Automated scheduling replaced manual email forwarding via Calendly integration
Improved open rates through timely, channel-specific messaging
Better segmentation through automated tagging by source, lifecycle, and company
Cross-team alignment with all activity visible across marketing, recruitment, and ops

Challenge

Member data migrated from legacy systems and spreadsheets into HubSpot. Contact model restructured. Custom properties built to capture the specific information the program needed.

Three workflows. Registration and welcome series for new members. Automated meeting reminders with personalized time slots and group numbers. Lifecycle communications that walked members through the full program.

Full email system designed. Templates, branding, automation sequences. The right message at the right time. No manual sends required.

Hands-on training for the Millennium Forum team. Detailed documentation. They managed the system independently after launch. I stayed on for support when needed.

Challenge

HubSpot was connected to Salesforce but wasn't really being used as a marketing platform. Salesforce handled recruitment and donors. HubSpot was more of a loose email tool, no structure for real campaign execution or clean segmentation.

Four recurring newsletters plus one-off sends for webinars, announcements, and outreach. 82,000 contacts. Most of the work was manual: rebuilding lists, recreating assets, double-checking fields before every send. Reporting was whatever HubSpot's subscription tier could show.

Approach

Four newsletter templates. Custom HTML/CSS. Each one tailored to its audience but built on shared structure and branding. The team stopped rebuilding from scratch every month. Production time dropped from 3 to 5 hours to about 60 to 90 minutes per send.

Campaign process standardized. Targeting, suppression, testing, QA, post-send review. Lists tightened. Pre-send checks formalized. Recurring and one-off sends followed the same workflow. No more depending on one person's memory.

Data hygiene workflows in HubSpot. Cleanup, field standardization, record hygiene. Property structure reorganized. Audience logic rebuilt. Lists became trustworthy. Pre-send troubleshooting dropped 65% in the first year.

HubSpot's reporting wasn't deep enough. Built a custom model: open rates, click rates, engagement trends, bounces, unsubscribes, historical comparisons. Reporting prep went from hours to under 45 minutes. Became the system for team reviews and year-end discussions.

50+ SOPs written. Campaign processes, template usage, segmentation rules, QA expectations, performance tracking. The team got a stable operating model. No tribal knowledge required.

Email Performance Analysis

A national nonprofit had a year of email data across three audience segments but no way to know what was actually working. I pulled the metrics, benchmarked them against industry averages, and built an interactive dashboard that turned raw numbers into clear priorities.

Challenge

The organization sent emails to three distinct audiences: SQLs, Customers, and MQLs. Each segment had different engagement patterns, but nobody had compared them side by side or against external benchmarks. Decisions were made on gut feel.

Open rates were climbing, but read rates were collapsing. Glance rates spiked over 300% across every segment. People were opening emails but not reading them. Without the data to prove it, the team kept writing long-form content that nobody finished.

There was no frame of reference. A 16% open rate sounds low, but is it? A 3.66% click rate sounds decent, but compared to what? The team had no way to know where they stood against other nonprofits their size.

Approach

I pulled a full year of email performance data across all three segments: open rates, click rates, click-to-open rates, bounce rates, unsubscribe rates, read rates, and glance rates. Every metric broken out by segment and compared year over year.

Benchmarked everything against national nonprofit averages from four industry sources: Neon One, MailerLite, M+R Benchmarks, and Nonprofit Tech for Good. Each metric rated as above, at, or below benchmark with context on what the gap means.

Built an interactive dashboard with Chart.js. Segment filters update every chart and KPI card in real time. Benchmark bands overlay the bar charts so you can see at a glance where you stand. The whole thing is a single HTML file, no server needed.

Translated the data gaps into six prioritized recommendations. High priority: clean the customer list (3.3x bounce ceiling), shorten email copy (read rates down 43%), and fix SQL subject lines (83% never open). Medium priority: optimize MQL CTAs, audit send cadence, and replicate the content patterns that drove the best engagement.

Results
Full benchmark scorecard across 5 core metrics for all 3 segments, rated against national averages
Interactive dashboard with segment filtering, YoY comparison charts, and benchmark overlay bands
Reading behavior shift identified — read rates down 20-43%, glance rates up 345-1,095% across segments
Customer segment CTOR at 14.97% — 1.5x the national benchmark, validating content strategy
6 prioritized recommendations with specific actions tied to benchmark gaps
List hygiene issue surfaced — 3.30% bounce rate in customer segment flagged before it damaged deliverability
